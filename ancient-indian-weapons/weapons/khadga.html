Khadga

Overview

The Khadga (Sanskrit: खड्ग) is a traditional double-edged, straight sword from the Indian subcontinent. Symbolizing power and divine justice, it was the most respected melee weapon. Its blade typically widens toward the tip, granting it a forward-leaning balance that results in devastating chopping and slashing capabilities.

Material & Metallurgy

While early Vedic iterations used bronze or primitive bloomery iron, the Khadga's lethal reputation peaked with the adoption of Wootz crucible steel. High-carbon Wootz allowed Indian smiths to forge a blade that maintained a razor-sharp edge without shattering against armor.

Through precise differential heat treatment (quenching and tempering), the edge became extremely hard, while the core and spine remained relatively soft and flexible, absorbing the intense shock of combat.

Metallurgical Profile

  • Material: Crucible Wootz Steel (1-2% Carbon)
  • Treatment: Differential Quenching
  • Key Trait: Edge retention paired with shock absorption

Role in Military Strategy

Within the Chaturanga (fourfold army), the Khadga was the indispensable sidearm of the Padati (Infantry) and Ashva (Cavalry). In standard Vyuha (formations), polearms like the Bhala held the line. However, when formations broke or entered close-quarter shock combat—often following a chariot or elephant charge—the Khadga was drawn. Its heavy, cleaving blows were designed specifically to sunder wooden shields and defeat the leather and mail armor of the era.

Historical & Cultural Context

Beyond the battlefield, the Khadga holds profound religious significance. In Hinduism, it is the weapon of Goddess Kali, symbolizing the severing of ignorance and ego. Royal consecrations involved the worship of the state's prime sword (Khadga Puja), recognizing it as the ultimate symbol of a King's martial duty (Kshatriya Dharma).
